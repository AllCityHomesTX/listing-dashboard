Why These Numbers Differ — And Why That Matters

🤖
Texas Doesn't Disclose Sale Prices Publicly
Texas is a "non-disclosure state." That means actual sale prices are NOT filed with the county — they're only available through the MLS. Zillow and Trulia cannot access this data directly, so their algorithms are working with incomplete information. This is the single biggest reason estimates in Texas are less reliable than in other states.
📊
Algorithms Miss What Makes This Home Unique
Zillow's Zestimate cannot see the greenbelt backing, the mature trees, the quality of the patio, or the condition of the interior. It works from tax records and square footage alone. The listing description says this home has been shown to be clean and in good condition — features an algorithm simply cannot price.
📅
Automated Estimates Lag Behind the Market
Zillow acknowledges a nationwide median error rate of 1.83% for active listings — that's a potential swing of ±$5,000 on this home. For off-market homes the error rate climbs to 7%. More importantly, algorithms react slowly to neighborhood-level shifts like the 11.5% decline in Burning Wood over 2 years.
✓
Even Zillow's Founder Sold His Own Home 40% Below Zestimate
In 2016, Zillow co-founder Spencer Rascoff sold his Seattle home for $1.05M — 40% below its own Zestimate of $1.75M. Zillow even shut down its own home-buying program in 2021 after losing $881M using its algorithm to price homes. These aren't algorithm critiques — they're Zillow's own public record.

Talking Points — If a Buyer Brings Up Zillow

"The Zillow estimate is $4,900 below list — does that mean it's overpriced?"
Not at all. A $4,900 gap is actually very close — well within Zillow's own published error range of 1.83% for active listings, which on this home equals ±$5,000. Texas also doesn't publicly disclose sale prices, so Zillow's algorithm is working with incomplete data. The list price was set using actual SABOR MLS sold data that Zillow simply doesn't have access to.
"Why is the Zillow range so wide — $240K to $294K?"
A wide range is Zillow's way of signaling uncertainty. It means the algorithm doesn't have enough data to be confident — which is exactly the Texas non-disclosure problem. The wider the range, the less Zillow actually knows. A narrow range from a CMA using real local data is far more meaningful.
"Redfin seems closer to list price — should I trust that more?"
Redfin does have MLS access in Texas which makes its estimates somewhat more accurate. Their own data shows 78247 homes sell for about 2% below list price in roughly 51 days. At $272,000, a 2% discount would bring an offer to about $266,560 — which would still be a fair negotiation. The listing is priced to reflect that reality.
"Trulia shows the same as Zillow — is that a coincidence?"
No coincidence — Zillow acquired Trulia in 2015. Both sites run on the same algorithm and share the same data. You're essentially looking at one source, not two independent opinions. Always check Redfin as well for a genuinely separate estimate.
